# 6. Discussion

## 6.1 The Colour Axis Is Where Photographic Aesthetics Fails to Transfer

The clearest signal in our results is that the attributes which lose the most relevance in art are precisely those governing colour and tone: Light ($T=+0.32$), ColorHarmony ($T=+0.24$), and VividColor ($T=+0.16$) are the three largest positive shifts, and all collapse from moderate correlation in photography to near-zero or negative correlation in art. This is not a random subset. In photography, exposure, colour harmony, and saturation are strong predictors of a well-received image because photographic practice treats them as technical objectives — a correctly exposed, colour-balanced photograph is, all else equal, a better photograph. In painting and illustration, the same axes are compositional *choices* rather than technical targets: a deliberately desaturated palette, a high-key or low-key tonal scheme, or a dissonant colour combination can be the very source of a work's aesthetic strength. The photographic colour vocabulary therefore does not merely weaken in art; for several attributes the correlation reverses sign, meaning a direction learned as "good" under photographic supervision points *against* aesthetic quality in the artistic domain.

The coverage analysis (Section 4.5) shows that this is not confined to individual attributes. Taken as a set, the readable attributes account for roughly 45% of the predictable aesthetic signal in photography but essentially none of it in art. The photographic aesthetic vocabulary, as a system, does not describe what makes an artwork good.

## 6.2 The Depth-of-Field and Object Reversals

The two attributes that behave in the opposite direction — depth of field ($T=-0.07$) and object emphasis ($T=-0.10$) — are the most informative results in the study, precisely because they were not predicted by the "everything transfers worse" intuition. Both correlate *more* strongly with aesthetic quality in art than in photography.

We interpret this as a difference in how deliberately each attribute is constructed in each medium. In photography, depth of field and subject isolation are frequently a by-product of equipment and shooting conditions — a wide aperture blurs a background whether or not the photographer intended it as an aesthetic statement — so their correlation with human aesthetic preference is diluted by incidental occurrences. In painting and digital art, by contrast, every region of focus and every emphasised object is placed by deliberate authorial decision; nothing is incidental. An artist who renders a subject in sharp focus against a loosely painted ground has made an aesthetic choice, and viewers respond to it as such. The attribute therefore carries *more* aesthetic signal in art, not less. This reversal suggests that "transfer failure" is not a uniform degradation but an attribute-specific realignment of what each axis means across media — a point with direct consequences for domain adaptation (Section 6.6).

## 6.3 Specialisation Has a Direction

The model grid (Section 4.x) makes the asymmetry concrete. The two photographic specialists collapse in the artistic domain — Q-Align falls from 0.817 on photographs to 0.126 on art, and Aesthetic Predictor V2.5 from 0.473 to 0.009 — while the art specialist, SAAN, reaches 0.472 on the same artworks. A model's aesthetic competence is thus not a general quantity that transfers across media; it is tied to the medium its supervision came from. Notably, even though SAAN's absolute in-domain performance (0.472) is modest, it still outperforms the strongest photographic model on art by a factor of roughly 3.7. On artworks, a specialised-but-weaker art model beats a strong-but-mismatched photographic model decisively.

The multi-domain model (ArtiMuse), trained on aesthetic data spanning photography, painting, 3D, and design, falls squarely between the two extremes on art: it reaches 0.199 on BAID, above both photographic specialists (0.126 and 0.009) yet far below the art specialist SAAN (0.472). This yields a clean ordering on the artistic domain — photo-specialist < multi-domain < art-specialist — and it is the central finding of the grid. Multi-domain training *does* partially recover artistic transfer relative to photo-only supervision, confirming that exposure to artistic data during training helps; but the large remaining gap to the art specialist shows that broad multi-domain coverage is not a substitute for domain-specific adaptation. This is precisely the regime a domain-adaptation method must improve upon.

## 6.4 The Label Ceiling

Several of our absolute correlations are low even in-domain, and this must be read against the ceiling of the artistic benchmark itself. SAAN, an art-specialised model, reaches only 0.472 on BAID's own test set, and the full 768-dimensional representations achieve $R^2_{\text{full}} \approx 0.10$ on BAID versus $\approx 0.45$ on AVA. Art aesthetics is intrinsically harder to predict, in part because BAID's labels derive from contest voting that reflects thematic fit and popularity alongside aesthetic merit. This is why we report transfer as a *shift* $T_a$ and coverage as a *ratio* rather than relying on raw correlation magnitudes: both quantities normalise away the differing ceilings of the two domains, so that our conclusions concern the *relative* behaviour of each attribute across media rather than absolute predictability. The pre-registered decision to demote raw $\Delta$ in favour of shift, ratio, and rank-agreement statistics was made precisely to avoid over-reading these low absolute numbers.

## 6.5 Course Learning Reflection

This study is an application of two modules of the course. From **Visual Intelligence**, we drew the method of *linear probing* on frozen representations — training a low-capacity linear model on fixed features to diagnose what information a representation linearly exposes, without altering the representation itself. From **Multimodal Intelligence**, we drew both the models (CLIP and SigLIP2, whose image–text contrastive pretraining aligns visual and linguistic representation) and the conceptual framing that a single visual representation may encode different notions of quality for different media. The exercise reinforced a lesson that the modules present in the abstract: a frozen representation is not a neutral container but carries the inductive biases of its training distribution, and those biases surface sharply the moment the representation is applied across a domain boundary. Building the analysis also made concrete the practical machinery behind these modules — feature extraction pipelines, the role of preprocessing, rank-based evaluation, and the discipline of pre-registration to keep an empirical study honest.

## 6.6 Limitations and Future Work

Our study has several limitations. First, the linear probe is deliberately low-capacity; a non-linear probe might recover some attribute signal that a linear read misses, though at the cost of the clean "present in the representation" interpretation. Second, the four unreadable attributes — all spatial and compositional — are invisible to our analysis not because they are unimportant to art but because pooled global features discard spatial layout; a spatially-aware probe is a natural extension. Third, our artistic domain is a single dataset (BAID), and its contest-derived labels impose the ceiling discussed above; replication on other artistic benchmarks would strengthen the conclusions.

The most direct continuation is prescriptive rather than diagnostic. This paper establishes *what* fails to transfer (the colour-tone axes), *what* strengthens in art (depth of field, object emphasis), and *how* to measure transfer (the shift statistic and coverage ratio). A domain-adaptation method for artistic aesthetic assessment can therefore be designed to target exactly the non-transferring axes while preserving those that already strengthen, and its success can be measured with the very instruments developed here — the same probes, the same shift, before and after adaptation. The diagnosis reported in this paper is, in that sense, the specification for the treatment that follows.
