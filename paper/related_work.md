# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Image Aesthetic Assessment

Image aesthetic assessment (IAA) seeks to predict human aesthetic preference from raw pixels, and its trajectory closely follows the course's progression from **Structural** to **Visual** and **Multimodal Intelligence**. Early work regressed a single aesthetic score from convolutional features, treating the problem as a supervised prediction task on large photographic datasets. The field's central benchmark, AVA [3], provides roughly 250,000 photographs with distributional aesthetic ratings, and virtually all subsequent progress in general IAA has been measured on it. AADB [1] refined this paradigm by pairing overall scores with eleven interpretable photographic attributes — colour harmony, depth of field, rule of thirds, and so on — enabling attribute-level rather than purely holistic analysis; we use exactly these attributes as the vocabulary probed in this work.

More recently, multimodal large language models have been repurposed as aesthetic scorers. Q-Align [Q-Align, ICML 2024] casts scoring as discrete rating-token prediction and reads out a continuous score from token probabilities, achieving strong performance on photographic IAA. ArtiMuse [ArtiMuse, 2025] extends this "token-as-score" idea on an InternVL-3 backbone and is trained on multi-domain aesthetic data spanning photography, painting, design, and 3D content. These models represent the current frontier of general and multi-domain aesthetic scoring, and we include representatives of both in our evaluation grid.

## 2.2 Artistic Image Aesthetics

A parallel line of work recognises that artistic images are judged by criteria that need not coincide with those of photography. BAID [2] introduced a large-scale dataset of artworks with human aesthetic ratings, together with SAAN, a style-aware network designed specifically for artistic aesthetic assessment. The very existence of art-specific architectures and datasets reflects an implicit assumption that photographic aesthetic models do not transfer to art. Our work makes this assumption explicit and measurable: rather than proposing another art-specific model, we quantify *which components* of photographic aesthetics fail to transfer and by how much, providing the diagnostic basis such specialised models implicitly rely on.

## 2.3 Probing Frozen Representations

Our methodology draws directly on the linear-probing tradition from the **Visual Intelligence** module. Linear probing trains a low-capacity linear model on top of frozen features to test what information a representation linearly exposes, without modifying the representation itself. The technique has been widely used to interpret self-supervised and vision–language encoders, on the principle that a property recoverable by a linear read is genuinely present in the representation rather than constructed by a high-capacity head. We apply this tradition in a novel comparative setting: rather than probing for object categories or semantic attributes within one domain, we train aesthetic-attribute probes in a photographic domain and transport them, unchanged, across a medium boundary into art.

## 2.4 Cross-Domain Transfer of Aesthetics

Prior studies of cross-domain aesthetics have largely operated at the level of whole-model performance: a scorer trained on one dataset is evaluated on another, and a drop in correlation is reported. Such end-to-end comparisons establish *that* aesthetic models degrade out of domain, but they cannot identify *which* aesthetic factor failed to carry over, because the output is a single opaque score. Our contribution is to decompose this transfer gap attribute by attribute. By probing individual, interpretable attributes and measuring each one's shift across media, we convert a global "art is different" observation into a structured, per-attribute account — separating attributes that lose relevance, those that reverse, and those the representation cannot read at all. To our knowledge this attribute-level, cross-architecture treatment of photographic-to-artistic aesthetic transfer has not been reported previously, and it is what distinguishes our study from both whole-model transfer comparisons and single-domain probing analyses.
